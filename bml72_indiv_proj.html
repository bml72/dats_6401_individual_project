This project attempts to compare the military, healthcare and educational expenditure of at least the top ten nations in the world. The data for this project spans the years 2013 through 2017 and focuses on the 19 countries that make up the current G-20. Because the European Union is considered a member of the G-20 and includes countries that are also part of the G-20, the European Union was excluded from this project (hence, 19 countries instead of 20). The military spending data was sourced from the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), while the healthcare and educational spending data were sourced from the World Bank.

It is important to note that while data for military, healthcare and educational expenditure was readily available, the educational data sourced from the World Bank contained many missing values for the countries included in this project. The reason for choosing the years 2013 through 2017 is because these years contained the greatest amount of available data on education. However, countries like Canada and China do not have any educational expenditure data in the chosen dataset for the last ten years. The visualizations included in this project include all available data from the sources used.

The general layout of this project includes an introduction to GDP by country, graphics of the different expenditure categories, and a comparison of country GDP to corresponding expenditure data. Analyses are provided for each graphic, and the process is repeated for per capita data. The changes in data are compared between years, and the fasted growing countries in healthcare and educational spending are decided. Additional comments are included in the last section of the webpage. The Google Charts API was used to construct the visualizations displayed in this project; credit for the visualizations can be found here.

Population and Per Capita Assessment

Now that GDP and the diferent expenditure categories have been effectively compared, the values will be assessed on a per capita scale. In essence, per capita scales expenses to a 'per-person' level; this will help erase bias for countries who boast a large population differential. Before per capita values are compared, it is important to gain a sense of population size for the countries in question.

World Map of Population Size

Analysis

The disparity in population size is clear: China and India possess the largest populations with over a billion each, due to their orange-reddish pigment. The US, Russia, Indonesia, Mexico and Japan remain distant in the hundreds of millions and display a solid orange pigment. Some of the less populated countries to consider are Saudi Arabia, France, and Germany, which sit in the tens of millions.

Fastest Growing Countries

This section explores trends between years of data to determine the fastest growing countries in healthcare and education. Originally, I'd tried including line charts of growth from year to year in fixed value in percentage, but the output messy and unhelpful. Additionally, due to the large number of API calls on this website, I often receive Uncaught Error response status 429(), which results from too many requests in a certain period of time. Because the output of the average growth histograms above were much cleaner, more helpful, and resulted in less 429() errors, I am excluding the line charts that show changes from year to year.

Please make a selection from the dropdown menu to view growth in terms of a specific type and value.

Analysis

In terms of fixed value, the United States leads the group in healthcare by a longshot, with an average growth of $149 billion per year. China is second behind the US but far ahead of the other countries with a measly $45 billion per year. In terms of percentage, Saudi Arabia of all countries leads the group in average growth of healthcare spending with 10.47%. China is second with 8.97%, while the US sits at sixth place with 5.10%. This is important because although the US spends much more on healthcare than any other country considered, their percentage growth is comparatively low. The fact that Saudi Arabia is in first in terms of percentage means they are (on average) spending more and more on healthcare each year (proportionately more than the US). In terms of growth in educational spending, there is some confusion. Although the US appears to possess the greatest decline in fixed value educational spending, this comes as a result of missing data. Because there was no data on US educational spending in 2015, 2016, and 2017, the jump from 2014 data ($869 billion) to 2015 data (missing; $0.00) appears extremely negative. Additionally, although France appears to boast the most positive average growth in educational spending, data for educational spending in France was available only in 2017. With this and other missing data in mind, South Africa appears to be the only country that boasts realistic positive growth in fixed value, with an average growth of $5.3 billion per year). However, in terms of percentage growth Argentina places first with a 4.87% average growth per year. Argentina also has data for every year considered.

Additional Comments

Perhaps I should address the elephant in the room. The United States spends by far the most money on military, healthcare, and education out of all countries in the G-20. The US possesses the greatest GDP, military expenditure, healthcare expenditure, educational expenditure*, per capita GDP, and per capita healthcare. Although the US does not lead in per capita military or educational expenditure, the fact that this country spends roughly $679 billion on military and $2 trillion on healthcare each year is insane. Although China appeared to boast large values at the general level, these values are biased. China's per capita GDP is among the lowest due to the fact that their population is over a billion (over three times as large as the US). However, one title that the US does not hold is the fastest growing country. In terms of healthcare expenditure, Saudi Arabia is the fastest growing country. In terms of educational expenditure, Argentina holds the title. Although the US holds the title of fastest growing country in terms of fixed value, they lack in terms of percentage growth. This is due to the fact that while the US has so much money on hand, their spending growth in comparison to previous years is lower than other countries. It's also important to note that at the per capita level, Saudi Arabia spends the most on military expenditure. The Saudia Arabian military is notorious for being one of the best-funded militaries in the world, so this doesn't come as much of a surprise. Due to their proportionately small population, Saudi Arabia did not appear to spend a lot on the general level in comparison to the US, whose population is roughly 10 times larger.

*educational expenditure data was sparsely available for certain countries.
